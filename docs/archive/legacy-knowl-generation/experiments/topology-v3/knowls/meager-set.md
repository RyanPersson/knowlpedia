---
title: "Meager set"
description: "A subset that is a countable union of nowhere dense sets."
---

A subset $M\subseteq X$ of a {{{< knowl id="topological-space" section="topology-core" text="topological space" >}}} $X$ is **meager** (or **of first category**) in $X$ if there exist {{{< knowl id="nowhere-dense-set" text="nowhere dense sets" >}}} $N_1,N_2,\dots\subseteq X$ such that
$$M=\bigcup_{n\in\mathbb{N}} N_n.$$

Meager sets are regarded as “small” in {{{< knowl id="baire-space" text="Baire category" >}}}: in a Baire space, no nonempty open subset is meager. The complement of a meager set is a {{{< knowl id="residual-set" text="residual set" >}}}.

**Examples:**
- In $\mathbb{R}$ with the usual topology, the rationals $\mathbb{Q}$ are meager because $\mathbb{Q}$ is a countable union of singletons, and each singleton is nowhere dense.
