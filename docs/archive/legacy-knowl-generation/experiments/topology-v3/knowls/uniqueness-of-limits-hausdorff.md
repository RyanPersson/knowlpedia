---
title: "Uniqueness of limits in Hausdorff spaces"
description: "In a Hausdorff space, a convergent sequence has at most one limit."
---

**Uniqueness of limits in Hausdorff spaces:** Let $X$ be a {{{< knowl id="hausdorff-space" text="Hausdorff space" >}}}, and let $(x_n)$ be a sequence in $X$. If $(x_n)$ has {{{< knowl id="limit-of-a-sequence" section="topology-metric" text="limit" >}}} $x$ and also has limit $y$, then $x=y$.

This is a central reason the Hausdorff axiom is imposed: in non-Hausdorff spaces, {{{< knowl id="convergent-sequence" section="topology-metric" text="convergent sequences" >}}} can have multiple limits.

**Examples:**
- In the indiscrete topology on a set with at least two points, every sequence converges to every point, so limits are never unique.
