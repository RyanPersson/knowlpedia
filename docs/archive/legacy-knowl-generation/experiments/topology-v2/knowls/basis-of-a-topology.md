---
title: "Basis of a topology"
description: "A collection of sets that generates a topology by taking unions."
---

A **basis of a topology** on a set $X$ is a collection $\mathcal{B}$ of subsets of $X$ such that:

1. For every $x\in X$, there exists $B\in\mathcal{B}$ with $x\in B$.
2. If $B_1,B_2\in\mathcal{B}$ and $x\in B_1\cap B_2$, then there exists $B_3\in\mathcal{B}$ with $x\in B_3\subseteq B_1\cap B_2$.

The {{< knowl id="topology-generated-by-basis" text="topology generated by" >}} $\mathcal{B}$ consists of all {{< knowl id="union" section="shared-foundations" text="unions" >}} of elements of $\mathcal{B}$; the resulting open sets are exactly those unions.

**Examples:**
- In $\mathbb{R}$ (usual topology), the open intervals $(a,b)$ form a basis.
- In a {{< knowl id="metric-space" section="topology-metric" text="metric space" >}}, the collection of open balls $B(x,r)$ is a basis for the metric topology.
- On any set $X$, the collection of all singletons $\{\{x\}:x\in X\}$ is a basis for the discrete topology.
