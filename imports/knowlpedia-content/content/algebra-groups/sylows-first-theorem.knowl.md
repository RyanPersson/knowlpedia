**Sylow's First Theorem.**
Let $G$ be a finite [[algebra-groups/group|group]], and write $|G| = p^{a}m$ where $p$ is prime and $p \nmid m$. Then $G$ has a [[algebra-groups/subgroup|subgroup]] of order $p^{a}$. Any subgroup of order $p^{a}$ is called a [[algebra-groups/sylow-subgroup|Sylow p-subgroup]].

Sylow's first theorem vastly strengthens [[algebra-groups/cauchys-theorem-groups|Cauchy's theorem]] (the case $a=1$). Together with [[algebra-groups/sylows-second-theorem|Sylow's second theorem]] and [[algebra-groups/sylows-third-theorem|Sylow's third theorem]], it controls the existence and placement of maximal [[algebra-groups/p-group|p-groups]] inside $G$.
