## Definition (entropy formulation)

For every equilibrium [[thermodynamics/thermodynamic-state|thermodynamic state]] of a [[thermodynamics/thermodynamic-system|thermodynamic system]], the second law asserts the existence of a state function $S$ called the [[thermodynamics/thermodynamic-entropy|thermodynamic entropy]] such that, for any [[thermodynamics/thermodynamic-process|thermodynamic process]] taking the system from equilibrium state $A$ to equilibrium state $B$,
$$
S(B)-S(A)\;\ge\;\int_A^B \frac{\delta Q}{T_{\mathrm{b}}}.
$$

Here $\delta Q$ is the (inexact) heat absorbed by the system (see [[thermodynamics/heat-inexact-differential|heat]]), and $T_{\mathrm{b}}$ is the [[thermodynamics/temperature-thermo|temperature]] at the boundary where that heat is exchanged (often set by a [[thermodynamics/thermal-reservoir|thermal reservoir]]).

Equality holds if and only if the process is [[thermodynamics/reversible-process|reversible]]; strict inequality signals [[thermodynamics/irreversible-process|irreversibility]].

A particularly transparent form is obtained by treating “system + [[thermodynamics/surroundings-environment|surroundings]]” as an [[thermodynamics/isolated-system|isolated system]]: then the second law becomes
$$
\Delta S_{\text{total}} \ge 0,
$$
so the total entropy cannot decrease.

## Physical interpretation

The second law is the thermodynamic expression of an “arrow of time”: macroscopic processes have a preferred direction because irreversibility produces entropy. Energy conservation alone (the [[thermodynamics/first-law-thermodynamics|first law]]) does not forbid processes that run “backwards,” but the second law does.

Operationally, the law is equivalent to the impossibility of certain cyclic devices, captured by the [[thermodynamics/kelvin-planck-statement|Kelvin–Planck statement]] and the [[thermodynamics/clausius-statement-second-law|Clausius statement]]. The mathematical backbone connecting these formulations is the [[thermodynamics/clausius-inequality|Clausius inequality]].

## Key relations and consequences

- **Entropy balance / entropy production.** Any process can be written as
  $$
  \Delta S = \int \frac{\delta Q}{T_{\mathrm{b}}} + S_{\mathrm{gen}},
  $$

  where $S_{\mathrm{gen}}\ge 0$ is entropy generated by irreversibility (and $S_{\mathrm{gen}}=0$ characterizes reversibility).

- **Isolated and adiabatic implications.** For an [[thermodynamics/isolated-system|isolated system]], $\Delta S\ge 0$. For a closed system insulated by an [[thermodynamics/adiabatic-wall|adiabatic wall]] (so $\delta Q=0$), one likewise has $\Delta S\ge 0$.

- **Free-energy monotonicity (common equilibrium criteria).** For a closed system in contact with a thermal reservoir at fixed $T$, the [[thermodynamics/helmholtz-free-energy|Helmholtz free energy]] (built from [[thermodynamics/internal-energy-thermo|internal energy]] and entropy) satisfies $\Delta F\le 0$ for spontaneous changes at fixed $T,V,N$. At fixed $T,p,N$, the [[thermodynamics/gibbs-free-energy|Gibbs free energy]] (built from [[thermodynamics/enthalpy|enthalpy]] and entropy) satisfies $\Delta G\le 0$.

- **Statistical-mechanical viewpoint.** Many microscopic derivations connect “entropy never decreases” to the nonnegativity of information measures such as [[probability/relative-entropy-kl-divergence|relative entropy (KL divergence)]] (see also [[probability/gibbs-inequality-kl|Gibbs inequality]]) and to the identification of $S/k_B$ with an entropy-like quantity (compare [[probability/shannon-entropy|Shannon entropy]]).
