Let \(K\) be a [[algebra-rings/field|field]] and let \(S\subseteq K[x]\) be a set of polynomials. Suppose \(L\) and \(L'\) are [[algebra-fields-galois/splitting-field|splitting fields]] of \(S\) over \(K\).

**Theorem (uniqueness up to \(K\)-isomorphism).**
There exists a \(K\)-[[algebra-fields-galois/field-embedding|embedding]] \(\varphi:L\to L'\) whose image is all of \(L'\); in particular, \(\varphi\) is an isomorphism of fields fixing \(K\) pointwise. Thus splitting fields of the same data are unique up to \(K\)-isomorphism.

A common strengthening: if one fixes an [[algebra-fields-galois/algebraic-closure|algebraic closure]] \(\overline K\) and realizes both \(L\) and \(L'\) as subfields of \(\overline K\) generated by all roots of \(S\), then \(L=L'\) as subfields of \(\overline K\). This is the “uniqueness inside a fixed closure” version (compare [[algebra-fields-galois/splitting-field-existence-uniqueness|existence and uniqueness of splitting fields]]).

### Examples

1. **Choice of square root does not matter.**
   Over \(K=\mathbb{Q}\), the polynomial \(x^2-2\) has roots \(\pm\sqrt2\). The splitting field generated by \(\sqrt2\) is \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2)\), and the splitting field generated by \(-\sqrt2\) is the same field. The \(K\)-automorphism \(\sqrt2\mapsto -\sqrt2\) exhibits the uniqueness.

2. **Cubic splitting fields.**
   For \(f=x^3-2\in\mathbb{Q}[x]\), any splitting field over \(\mathbb{Q}\) is \(K\)-isomorphic to \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2},\zeta_3)\), even if one starts by adjoining a different real cube root or different primitive cube root of unity. This matches the fact that a normal extension is characterized as a splitting field (see [[algebra-fields-galois/normality-splitting-field|normality and splitting fields]]).

3. **Finite fields.**
   Any two fields of size \(p^n\) are isomorphic (see [[algebra-fields-galois/finite-field-existence-uniqueness|existence and uniqueness of finite fields]]). In particular, if \(L\) is the splitting field over \(\mathbb{F}_p\) of an irreducible degree-\(n\) polynomial, then \(L\cong \mathbb{F}_{p^n}\) regardless of which irreducible polynomial one starts with.
