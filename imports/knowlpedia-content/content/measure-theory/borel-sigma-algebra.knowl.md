A **Borel sigma-algebra** on a [[topology/topological-space|topological space]] $X$ is the [[measure-theory/sigma-algebra|sigma-algebra]] $\mathcal B(X)$ generated by the [[topology/open-set|open sets]] of $X$, i.e. the smallest sigma-algebra that contains every open subset of $X$.

Equipping $X$ with $\mathcal B(X)$ turns it into a [[measure-theory/measurable-space|measurable space]] in a way that is compatible with topology: many naturally occurring functions (especially continuous ones) become [[measure-theory/measurable-function|measurable]] with respect to Borel sigma-algebras.

**Examples:**
- On $\mathbb R$ with its usual topology, $\mathcal B(\mathbb R)$ is generated by open [[real-analysis/interval|intervals]] such as $(a,b)$.
- If $X$ has the discrete topology (all sets are open), then $\mathcal B(X)=\mathcal P(X)$, the [[shared-foundations/power-set|power set]].
- If $X$ has the trivial topology $\{\varnothing, X\}$, then $\mathcal B(X)=\{\varnothing, X\}$.
