A finite-dimensional Lie algebra $\mathfrak g$ (over a field, typically of characteristic $0$) is **simple** if:

1. $\mathfrak g$ is not abelian (see [[lie-groups/abelian-lie-algebra|abelian Lie algebra]]), and
2. the only ideals in $\mathfrak g$ are $0$ and $\mathfrak g$ itself (see [[lie-groups/ideal-lie-algebra|ideal]]).

Simplicity is the Lie-algebra analogue of “no nontrivial normal subgroups” in group theory, but formulated in terms of invariant subspaces under the adjoint action (see [[lie-groups/adjoint-representation-of-a-lie-algebra|adjoint representation]]). A basic immediate consequence is that any nonzero homomorphism out of a simple Lie algebra is injective (see [[lie-groups/lie-algebra-homomorphism|Lie algebra homomorphism]]).

Simple Lie algebras are the building blocks of semisimple ones: every [[lie-groups/semisimple-lie-algebra|semisimple Lie algebra]] decomposes as a direct sum of simple ideals (see [[lie-groups/semisimple-direct-sum-simple|semisimple direct sum simple]]). Over $\mathbb C$, the complete list of finite-dimensional simple Lie algebras is given by the [[lie-groups/classification-simple-lie-algebras|Cartan–Killing classification]].
