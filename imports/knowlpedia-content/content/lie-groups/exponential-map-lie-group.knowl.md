Let $G$ be a [[fiber-bundles/lie-group|Lie group]] with Lie algebra $\mathfrak g=\mathrm{Lie}(G)$ (see [[lie-groups/lie-algebra-of-a-lie-group|Lie algebra of a Lie group]]).

For each $X\in\mathfrak g$, there is a unique [[lie-groups/one-parameter-subgroup|one-parameter subgroup]] $\gamma_X:\mathbb R\to G$ whose tangent at $0$ equals $X$ (compare [[lie-groups/exponential-one-parameter-subgroup-lemma|exponential/one-parameter subgroup lemma]]). The **exponential map** is
\[
\exp:\mathfrak g\to G,\qquad \exp(X):=\gamma_X(1).
\]
Equivalently, $\gamma_X(t)=\exp(tX)$ for all $t\in\mathbb R$.

## Functoriality
If $\Phi:G\to H$ is a [[lie-groups/lie-group-homomorphism|Lie group homomorphism]], then differentiation gives a Lie algebra map $d\Phi_e$ (see [[lie-groups/differential-is-lie-algebra-homomorphism|differential is a Lie algebra homomorphism]]), and exponentials intertwine:
\[
\Phi(\exp_G X)=\exp_H(d\Phi_e X).
\]

## Matrix groups: concrete formula
If $G\subseteq GL(n,\mathbb F)$ is a matrix Lie group, then for $X\in\mathfrak g\subseteq \mathfrak{gl}_n(\mathbb F)$,
\[
\exp(X)=e^X:=\sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{X^k}{k!}.
\]
For example, in [[lie-groups/example-u1-circle|$U(1)$]], this recovers $t\mapsto e^{it}$; in [[lie-groups/example-so3|$SO(3)$]], exponentials of skew-symmetric matrices are rotation matrices.

## Local behavior
The exponential map is always a local diffeomorphism at $0$ (see [[lie-groups/exponential-local-diffeomorphism|exponential is a local diffeomorphism]]). The local inverse is the [[lie-groups/logarithm-map|logarithm map]].

Transporting multiplication through $\log$ gives the local group law on $\mathfrak g$ described by the [[lie-groups/baker-campbell-hausdorff-formula|Baker–Campbell–Hausdorff formula]].

## Global remarks
- $\exp$ need not be surjective in general (not every element must lie on a 1-parameter subgroup).
- For [[lie-groups/compact-lie-group|connected compact Lie groups]], $\exp$ is surjective: every element lies in a maximal torus (compare [[lie-groups/maximal-torus-theorem|maximal torus theorem]]) and exponentials are surjective on tori (see [[lie-groups/example-torus|the torus example]]).

**Context.** The exponential map is the primary bridge between the linear object $\mathfrak g$ and the nonlinear group $G$, converting Lie-algebraic computations into local (and sometimes global) statements about the Lie group.
