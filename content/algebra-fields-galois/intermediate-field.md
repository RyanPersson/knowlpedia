---
title: "Intermediate field"
description: "A field F with K ⊆ F ⊆ L inside a field extension L/K."
---

**Definition.** Let \(L/K\) be a {{< knowl id="field-extension" text="field extension" >}}. An **intermediate field** (or **subextension**) is a field \(F\) such that
\[
K \subseteq F \subseteq L.
\]
Equivalently, \(F\) is a subfield of \(L\) that contains \(K\).

Intermediate fields are the “levels” in a {{< knowl id="tower-of-fields" text="tower of fields" >}} and interact with degrees via the {{< knowl id="tower-law" text="tower law" >}} when the degrees are finite.

**See also.** {{< knowl id="degree-of-extension" text="degree of an extension" >}}, {{< knowl id="galois-correspondence" text="Galois correspondence" >}}.

**Examples.**
1. In \(\mathbb{Q}\subseteq \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2,\sqrt3)\), the fields \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2)\), \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt3)\), and \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt6)\) are intermediate fields.
2. In \(\mathbb{R}\subseteq \mathbb{C}\), there is no proper intermediate field: the only intermediate fields are \(\mathbb{R}\) and \(\mathbb{C}\) (since \([\mathbb{C}:\mathbb{R}]=2\)).
3. If \(L=\mathbb{F}_{p^6}\) and \(K=\mathbb{F}_p\), then \(\mathbb{F}_{p^2}\) and \(\mathbb{F}_{p^3}\) are intermediate fields (unique of those sizes).
