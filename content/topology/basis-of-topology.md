---
title: "Basis of a topology"
description: "A collection of sets whose unions give all open sets."
---

A **basis of a topology** on a set $X$ is a collection $\mathcal{B}$ of subsets of $X$ such that:
1. for every $x\in X$ there exists $B\in\mathcal{B}$ with $x\in B$,
2. if $x\in B_1\cap B_2$ with $B_1,B_2\in\mathcal{B}$, then there exists $B_3\in\mathcal{B}$ such that $x\in B_3\subseteq B_1\cap B_2$.

The topology generated by $\mathcal{B}$ is the collection of all unions of elements of $\mathcal{B}$; these unions are exactly the {{< knowl id="open-set" text="open sets" >}}. Bases are a standard way to specify a {{< knowl id="topology" text="topology" >}} efficiently and to test {{< knowl id="continuous-map" text="continuity" >}} using only basic open sets.

**Examples:**
- In $\mathbb{R}$ with the usual topology, the open intervals $(a,b)$ form a basis.
- In a {{< knowl id="metric-space" text="metric space" >}}, the family of {{< knowl id="open-ball" text="open balls" >}} forms a basis for the {{< knowl id="metric-induced-topology" text="metric-induced topology" >}}.
- In a product $X\times Y$ with the {{< knowl id="product-topology" text="product topology" >}}, the sets $U\times V$ with $U$ open in $X$ and $V$ open in $Y$ form a basis.
